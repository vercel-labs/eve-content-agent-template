# Newsletter best practices

A sourced checklist of current tactics for writing an email newsletter. Use it as a pre-flight
check, not a recipe — the house voice (first-person, plain, concrete) wins over any single rule.

## The subject line

- [ ] Earn the open with something specific and true. Front-load the message: most opens happen
      on a phone, where mobile inboxes show only the first ~33–43 characters before truncating.
- [ ] Keep it short. Mailchimp caps at 9 words / 60 characters; 30–50 characters is the widely
      cited sweet spot, and the shortest lines (often 2–4 words) tend to test best.
- [ ] At most one emoji, and use it to support a word, not replace it — it renders differently
      across systems. No more than three punctuation marks; punctuation walls read as spam.
- [ ] Don't title it "Issue #11" or "This month's newsletter". Name the one thing inside.

## The preheader

- [ ] Write the preheader on purpose. If you leave it blank, the client grabs the first text it
      finds — alt text, a nav link, the unsubscribe line — almost never the teaser you want.
- [ ] Extend the subject line, don't repeat it. If the subject teases, let the preheader explain;
      if the subject explains, let the preheader tease. They're a two-part hook.
- [ ] Front-load it: put the essential words in the first ~40 characters, since mobile clients
      often show only 30–40 and Gmail's app truncates near 35–40.

## The open

- [ ] Land the one idea in the first sentence or two. Readers fixate on the headline and the
      first line or two of a story, then scan; the primacy effect means the top is best retained.
- [ ] No "Welcome back to another edition of…", no "in this issue we'll cover…". Just start.

## One idea, scannable

- [ ] One main thing per issue. Curated links can follow, but the issue needs a spine — a single
      story or insight, often 300–600 words, beats a grab-bag of five tips for holding attention.
- [ ] People give a newsletter about 51 seconds after opening — roughly 200 words — and most
      scan rather than read. Write so the gist survives a scan.
- [ ] Most readers scan in an F-pattern: they read across the top, drop down, read a shorter line,
      then run down the left edge. Put the load-bearing words at the starts of lines and sections.
- [ ] Give each chunk one point and a meaningful subheading; subheadings are the most effective
      thing a scanner follows (the "layer-cake" pattern). Bold a few keywords so the bolded text
      alone tells a coherent story.
- [ ] Short paragraphs and single-idea bullets. In a bulleted list, the first item and the first
      words of each bullet get read most — front-load there too.
- [ ] Conversational but tight. Read it aloud; cut anything you'd never say to one person.

## The CTA

- [ ] One clear next step per issue — read, try, reply — not five competing asks. The inverted
      triangle (broad open narrowing to one action) is the layout built for a single CTA.
- [ ] Don't bury the CTA at the very bottom; many scanners never reach it. Keep button copy to
      two to five words.
- [ ] A genuine "hit reply and tell me…" both invites a real response and signals engagement to
      the mailbox provider. Ask because you want the answer, not as bait.

## Sender & format

- [ ] Use a recognizable, consistent from-name. Recognizing the sender is the single biggest
      reason people open — for ~88% of users it's the top factor, ahead of the subject line.
      "Sarah from Acme" adds a human touch once the brand is known; otherwise lead with the brand.
- [ ] Default to a clean, near-plain-text feel. In HubSpot's A/B tests, plain-text consistently
      beat HTML on opens and clicks — adding images cut clickthrough, and the more HTML-rich the
      email, the lower the open rate. Image-heavy templates also get filtered to Gmail's
      Promotions tab.
- [ ] Keep a healthy text-to-image ratio; a wall of images with little text trips spam filters
      and breaks when a client blocks images by default.
